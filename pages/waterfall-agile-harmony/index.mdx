# Add some waterfall to your agile


<img src={require('src/images/ForrestGump.png')} alt="Forrest Gump ponders agile development" />

> "Do we all have a destiny, or are we floating around accidental - like on a breeze. Maybe it's both"\
> &mdash; Forrest Gump

Every startup and tech team is going to say they run agile projects. And often times, that's used as an excuse to "wing it" with processes and legitimize building bad products. For example, some teams spend every sprint tackling one ad hoc feature or urgent bug after another, and an engineering manager might pretend they are "welcoming changing requirements" (the 2nd principle in the agile manifesto). Or, a team might build the wrong product, having skipped a lot of necessary UX and customer research, design review, and roadmap planning, and it's dismissed as "a necessary iteration of product development".

Teams that make these mistakes and forget to reflect on them are neglecting the last principle of agile development. To stop and reflect on outcomes then "tune and adjust" their behavior to make the team more effective. Sometimes, I promise, the adjustment is to incorporate a little more waterfall into the software development life cycle.

Use waterfall, just enough to spot future challenges early on. Then, the team may arrive at them with tactical solutions in mind. Have a roadmap in mind for the next couple quarters even if that roadmap has to change a little every sprint. Make the roadmap into a tree so that the trajectory changes when product decisions are made (and as we learn more and more about how the product might satisfy the customer). 

When people belittle waterfall, they will conjure up all the time wasted building the book of work and a gantt chart. But those things are not, in and of themselves, the biggest waste of resources. It is building, blindly, by the book, and arriving at a place where you built the wrong product, that's the tragic waste of time. But spending some time conceptualizing how you **might** build the entire product - as you would have it today - is still a useful step in an agile development process. It's part of the architecture planning process.

While some waterfall will help a team think ahead about looming architecture decision, it can also help provide clarity of vision to the product and leadership teams. Sales people can try selling the product for it's built, winning some early adopters or letters of intent. Product people can build prototypes to test on their audience well before features are underway. And, executives can better coordinate resources and staff knowing what plans lie ahead.  

There are some useful artifacts of the waterfall process that should be used to inform the agile one.
